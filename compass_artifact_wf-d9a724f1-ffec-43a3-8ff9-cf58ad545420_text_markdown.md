# Theoretical foundations for Trasgo: a rigorous survey

**Large language models can plausibly induce a compact, structured context codec from a handful of in-context examples — and a convergent body of formal results across optimization theory, information theory, geometric representation learning, and emergent communication now supplies the theoretical scaffolding for that claim.** This report assembles the most rigorous sources across seven foundational pillars, extracting precise theorems, proven bounds, and empirical results suitable for citation in a technical thesis. The evidence reveals that in-context learning implements gradient descent over implicit function spaces, that prediction and compression are formally equivalent, and that neural agents under information pressure converge to minimal-description protocols — all supporting the Trasgo architecture's core premise.

---

## 1. In-context learning provably implements gradient descent

The thesis that transformers perform implicit optimization during inference rests on a progression from empirical demonstration to formal proof. **Garg, Tsipras, Liang, and Valiant (NeurIPS 2022)** showed empirically that GPT-2-style transformers trained on random function instances match the optimal least-squares estimator for linear functions, Lasso for sparse linear functions, and gradient-descent-trained networks for two-layer neural networks and decision trees. Their results are empirical, not formal theorems, but they established the research program.

The constructive equivalence came from **Von Oswald, Niklasson, Randazzo, Sacramento, Mordvintsev, Zhmoginov, and Vladymyrov (ICML 2023)**, who proved (Proposition 1) that a single linear self-attention layer with appropriately constructed weight matrices executes exactly one step of gradient descent on the MSE loss: the token update e_j ← (x_j, y_j − ΔW x_j) where ΔW = −(η/N) Σᵢ (Wxᵢ − yᵢ)xᵢᵀ. Multiple layers implement multiple GD steps. With MLP layers added, the construction extends to learning linear models on deep data representations, enabling nonlinear regression. The key constraint is **linear self-attention** — softmax is removed.

**Akyürek, Schuurmans, Andreas, Ma, and Zhou (ICLR 2023)** provided complementary constructive theorems. Their Theorem 1 shows a transformer can compute a single GD step with **constant layers and O(d) hidden dimension**, while Theorem 2 shows ridge regression via Sherman-Morrison iterative matrix inversion with layer count scaling with example count. Empirically, they found phase transitions: shallow models match GD, deeper models converge to Bayesian/ridge estimators.

**Bai, Chen, Wang, Xiong, and Mei (NeurIPS 2023)** achieved the strongest formal results. Their Corollary 5 proves near-optimal prediction: E[(ŷ − y)²] ≤ inf_w E[(wᵀx − y)²] + Õ(d/N), matching the minimax rate for linear regression. They proved transformers can implement least squares, ridge, Lasso, convex risk minimization for GLMs, and gradient descent on two-layer neural networks, all with **polynomial pretraining sample complexity** — the first such result. Their construction uses ReLU attention with O(log(1/ε)) layers. **Dai, Sun, Dong, Hao, Ma, Sui, and Wei (ACL Findings 2023)** established the dual-form connection: attention output decomposes as (W₀ + ΔW)·x where ΔW = V·Kᵀ, mirroring gradient-based fine-tuning.

The field then advanced from expressivity to **learnability**. **Ahn, Cheng, Daneshmand, and Sra (NeurIPS 2023)** proved that the global minimum of the ICL training objective for a single linear attention layer implements preconditioned gradient descent, with the preconditioner adapting to the input covariance — the first result showing GD *emerges* from training rather than just being expressible. **Mahankali, Hashimoto, and Ma (ICLR 2024)** independently proved that under isotropic Gaussians, the pre-training loss minimizer is exactly one GD step on least-squares. **Zhang, Frei, and Bartlett (JMLR 2024)** showed gradient flow with random initialization converges to the global minimum, achieving prediction error O(1/M + 1/N²).

The critical nonlinear extension came from **Cheng, Chen, and Sra (ICML 2024)**, who proved that nonlinear transformers implement **functional gradient descent in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space** (RKHS), where the kernel is determined by the attention activation. This is the first rigorous extension beyond linear functions: the activation function determines the function class that can be learned in-context, connecting to kernel regression and nonparametric methods. For the Trasgo thesis, this result is pivotal — it implies that transformers performing in-context codec induction are executing implicit optimization over a function space rich enough to encompass structured mappings.

## 2. The emergence debate and grammar induction thresholds

**Wei, Tay, Bommasani, Raffel, Zoph, and 11 others (TMLR 2022)** cataloged **137 emergent abilities** across GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, and PaLM, defining emergence as capabilities absent in smaller models but present in larger ones, with near-random performance until a critical threshold around **10²²–10²⁴ training FLOPs** (~100B parameters). The counter-argument from **Schaeffer, Miranda, and Koyejo (NeurIPS 2023)** demonstrated that >92% of claimed emergent abilities on BIG-Bench appeared under just two discontinuous metrics (Multiple Choice Grade and Exact String Match). Switching to linear metrics (token edit distance, Brier score) often reveals smooth improvement.

The resolution is nuanced. **Du, Zeng, Dong, and Tang (NeurIPS 2024)** showed that emergence persists even with continuous metrics when viewed through **pre-training loss** rather than model size — sharp performance jumps occur at specific loss thresholds regardless of metric choice. This reframing is important for Trasgo: codec induction capability would emerge when pre-training loss drops below a specific threshold, independent of the particular model scale that achieves it.

**Chen, Shwartz-Ziv, Cho, Leavitt, and Saphra (ICLR 2024)** provided the most direct evidence for grammar-related phase transitions. In BERT-style models, **Syntactic Attention Structure** emerges abruptly during training, precipitating grammatical capabilities — a genuine phase transition, not a metric artifact. Suppressing SAS during training prevents linguistic capabilities from emerging. **Olsson, Elhage, Nanda, and 24 others (Transformer Circuits Thread, 2022)** discovered that induction heads — the foundation of in-context pattern matching — form through a sharp phase change at ~1-2% of training.

**Arora and Goyal (arXiv 2023)** provide the strongest theoretical framework for predicting codec induction emergence. Their key formal result: competence at k-tuple skill compositions emerges at **the same scaling rate** as individual skill competence, with k' roughly doubling per 10× model scale. Grammar induction decomposes into sub-skills (pattern recognition, structural generalization, rule composition, abstraction), and the theory predicts the combined ability emerges once components individually reach competence — without requiring grammar induction training examples.

For Trasgo specifically, **Qiu et al. (NeurIPS 2023)** on Grammar Prompting showed that LLMs can learn to use BNF grammars provided in-context to generate valid DSL outputs. Notably, grammar prompting did not improve performance for DSLs already in pretraining data (SQL, regex) but significantly helped for novel DSLs — suggesting that grammar induction is a genuine in-context learning capability rather than memorization.

## 3. Prediction and compression are formally equivalent

The information-theoretic backbone of Trasgo rests on a precise mathematical identity. For any probabilistic model ρ assigning P(xᵢ | x<ᵢ) to each symbol, arithmetic coding produces a lossless code of length **ℓ(x₁:ₙ) = Σᵢ ⌈−log₂ ρ(xᵢ | x<ᵢ)⌉ + O(1)** bits. Conversely, any lossless compressor defines a probability distribution via ρ̂(b | x<ᵢ) = 2^{ℓ(x<ᵢ) − ℓ(x<ᵢ · b)}. Therefore, **optimal prediction and optimal compression are equivalent** — a result formalized by Hutter (2005) and made central to the LLM literature by **Delétang, Ruoss, Duquenne, Catt, Genewein, Mattern, Grau-Moya, Wenliang, Aitchison, Orseau, Hutter, and Veness (ICLR 2024)**.

Delétang et al. demonstrated this empirically: **Chinchilla 70B compresses English text (enwik9) to 8.3%** of raw size, versus 32.3% for gzip and 23.0% for LZMA2. Remarkably, it compresses ImageNet patches to 43.4% (vs. PNG at 58.5%) and LibriSpeech audio to 16.4% (vs. FLAC at 30.3%), despite being trained exclusively on text. Since LLMs are trained to minimize cross-entropy loss, they are literally being trained as optimal compressors — and cross-entropy in bits per token *is* the achievable compression rate.

The entropy rate of English constrains any compression scheme. Shannon's 1951 estimate of **~1.0 bits per character (bpc)** has been refined by **Takahashi and Tanaka-Ishii (Entropy, 2018)** to **1.12 bpc** via neural language model extrapolation, and by **Ren, Takahashi, and Tanaka-Ishii (Entropy, 2019)** to **1.22 bpc** via large-scale cognitive experiment (683 subjects, 172,954 predictions). Modern LLMs achieve raw compression rates of **~0.66–0.96 bpc** on English Wikipedia — apparently below the entropy rate, but only because the model itself stores terabytes of learned regularities.

For Trasgo's context compression claim, the rate-distortion framework is essential. **Nagle, Girish, Bondaschi, Gastpar, Makkuva, and Kim (NeurIPS 2024)** formalized prompt compression as a linear program: D*(R) = inf E[d(Y, ϕ_LLM(M, Q))] subject to E[len(M)/len(X)] ≤ R. Their key finding: existing prompt compression methods (LLMLingua, Selective Context) operate **far from the theoretical optimum**, and query-aware compression significantly outperforms query-agnostic approaches. **Arda and Yener (arXiv 2025)** extended this to summarization via a Blahut-Arimoto-like algorithm, showing existing summarizers operate well above the computed lower bound.

## 4. Transformers can learn context-free grammars and induce DSLs

The formal expressiveness of transformers both enables and constrains codec induction. **Yun, Bhojanapalli, Rawat, Reddi, and Kumar (ICLR 2020)** proved that transformers with positional encodings are **universal approximators of arbitrary continuous sequence-to-sequence functions** on compact domains. This is necessary but not sufficient for grammar induction — the architecture is not the bottleneck.

**Allen-Zhu and Li (ICLR 2025)** provided the most relevant positive result in their "Physics of Language Models" series. They demonstrated that GPT-style models **accurately learn and reason over context-free grammars**, with internal hidden states precisely capturing CFG structure and attention patterns resembling CYK dynamic programming. This is direct evidence that transformers can represent hierarchical grammatical structure — the foundation of any codec or DSL.

The formal limits are characterized by **Strobl, Merrill, Weiss, Chiang, and Angluin (TACL 2024)** in their survey "What Formal Languages Can Transformers Express?" Softmax-attention transformers with O(1) precision lie within **DLOGTIME-uniform TC⁰** — they can express MAJORITY but not PARITY. The Chomsky hierarchy alone is insufficient; circuit complexity provides a better lens. **Delétang, Ruoss, Grau-Moya, Genewein, and others (ICLR 2023)** in "Neural Networks and the Chomsky Hierarchy" showed that pure transformers generalize only to regular languages for strict OOD tasks, with stack-augmented networks needed for context-free generalization. However, modern LLMs with autoregressive generation, chain-of-thought, and massive pretraining effectively handle context-free and limited context-sensitive patterns in practice.

The most directly relevant paper for Trasgo is **Wang, Wang, Wang, Cao, Saurous, and Kim (NeurIPS 2023)** on Grammar Prompting. They showed LLMs can: (1) observe input-output pairs, (2) **induce a specialized BNF grammar** from those examples, and (3) use that grammar to generate new outputs across semantic parsing (SMCalFlow, Overnight, GeoQuery), PDDL planning, and SMILES molecule generation. Grammar prompting is effectively chain-of-thought where the intermediate "thought" is a formal grammar. On the engineering side, **Willard and Louf (arXiv 2023)** and **Dong, Ruan, Cai, Lai, Pan, Zhao, and Chen (MLSys 2025)** showed that grammar-constrained decoding can enforce any induced grammar with minimal overhead, achieving CFG-level expressiveness at FSM-level performance for ~99% of tokens.

## 5. Fiber bundles provide the geometric formalism for structured representations

The geometric framework for Trasgo draws on **Bronstein, Bruna, Cohen, and Veličković (arXiv 2021)**, whose "5G" Geometric Deep Learning framework unifies CNNs, GNNs, and Transformers under symmetry and equivariance principles. The "Gauges" pillar explicitly deploys fiber bundle formalism: feature maps are **sections of associated vector bundles**, and gauge equivariance ensures outputs are independent of arbitrary local frame choices. The key abstraction: at each point in a domain (analogous to each token position), a structured feature vector lives in a "fiber" that transforms coherently under context changes.

**Cohen, Weiler, Kicanaoglu, and Welling (ICML 2019)** formalized gauge equivariant convolution on manifolds: [k ★ f](p) = ∫ K(v) ρ_in(g_{p←q}) f(q) dv, where g_{p←q} is parallel transport. Feature fields are sections of associated vector bundles E = P ×_ρ V, where P is the principal frame bundle. **Cohen, Geiger, and Weiler (NeurIPS 2019)** proved the "Convolution is all you need" theorem: the most general equivariant linear map between feature spaces is a generalized convolution with an equivariant kernel. **Weiler and Cesa (NeurIPS 2019)** showed all G-steerable kernel constraints can be solved analytically via irreducible representations.

The bridge to transformers exists: **He, Dong, and Wang (NeurIPS 2021)** introduced the **Gauge Equivariant Transformer (GET)**, the first work making self-attention gauge-equivariant, though applied to mesh data rather than language. **Liu (arXiv, December 2025)** proposed **FiberNet**, reformulating classification as geometric optimization on fiber bundles where categories form the base space, wavelet-transformed features lie in fibers, and a variational energy functional (attachment + tension + repulsion) is minimized over sections and Riemannian metrics. **Courts and Kvinge (ICLR 2022)** developed Bundle Networks exploiting local trivializations for many-to-one maps, and **Coda, Courts, and Kvinge et al. (PMLR 2022)** extended this to many-to-many maps via fiber bundle morphisms, explicitly motivating the framework with NLP sentiment analysis.

Critically, **no published work as of March 2026 directly formulates transformer internal context representations as sections of fiber bundles** — this represents a genuine contribution opportunity for the Trasgo thesis. The related work on sheaf neural networks (**Bodnar, Di Giovanni et al., 2022**; **Ayzenberg and Magai, arXiv 2025**) and categorical deep learning (**Gavranović, Lessard, Dudzik, Von Glehn, Araújo, and Veličković, ICML 2024**) provides additional algebraic-topological tools for formalizing how structured context representations compose and transform.

## 6. A compact codec minimizes description length

The MDL principle, formulated by **Rissanen (Automatica, 1978)** and comprehensively treated by **Grünwald (MIT Press, 2007)**, directly formalizes the Trasgo codec as a two-part code: Part 1 is the JSON schema (model), Part 2 is the populated JSON values (data given model). The total description length L(schema) + L(data|schema) should be shorter than the natural-language encoding for MDL to favor the codec. The theoretical underpinning is Kolmogorov complexity: as established by **Li and Vitányi (Springer, 4th ed. 2019)**, the invariance theorem guarantees K(x) is language-independent up to an additive constant, and any compressor provides an upper bound on K(x). A codec achieving shorter descriptions than natural language demonstrates lower upper bounds on the context's Kolmogorov complexity.

**Goldblum, Finzi, Rowan, and Wilson (ICML 2024)** proved that neural networks — including randomly initialized GPT-2/GPT-3 — exhibit **inherent simplicity bias** toward low-Kolmogorov-complexity outputs. Larger models show even stronger preference. This directly supports Trasgo: a compact JSON codec presents context in a low-complexity structured form aligned with LLMs' inductive preference. **Wan (arXiv 2025)** proved (Theorem 4.1) that LLM pre-training is equivalent to approaching **joint Kolmogorov complexity C(x,y)** from above, with scaling laws improving approximation of conditional complexity C(x|y). **Wan and Mei (arXiv 2025)** went further, proving two fundamental theorems: (1) LLM training computationally approximates the **Solomonoff prior** through loss minimization interpreted as program length optimization, and (2) LLM next-token prediction implements **approximate Solomonoff induction** — the theoretically optimal universal predictor.

**Blier and Ollivier (NeurIPS 2018)** demonstrated that despite overparameterization, deep networks compress training data in the MDL sense, with prequential coding yielding far better compression bounds than variational methods. **Pan, Wang, and Li (arXiv 2025)** used the Kolmogorov Structure Function to interpret LLM compression as two-part coding, showing low-capacity models capture syntax while higher-capacity models capture rarer knowledge — the "Syntax-Knowledge" hierarchy. For prompt compression specifically, **Mu, Li, and Goodman (NeurIPS 2023)** achieved **26× compression** of prompts into "gist tokens" with minimal quality loss, and the LLMLingua series (Jiang et al., EMNLP 2023; ACL 2024) achieves **20× hard-prompt compression** via perplexity-based token pruning. These results demonstrate massive redundancy in natural-language context — redundancy that a structured codec can eliminate at the representation level rather than requiring the LLM to compress it internally.

## 7. Multi-agent systems converge to compressed communication codes

The emergent communication literature provides strong evidence that neural agents under information pressure develop exactly the kind of compact, efficient protocols Trasgo proposes. **Kharitonov, Chaabouni, Bouchacourt, and Baroni (ICML 2020)** proved that emergent languages are subject to **entropy minimization pressure**: mutual information between inputs and messages is minimized within the range required for task success. Agents develop codes that are **(nearly) as simple as the task allows**, with pressure amplified as channel discreteness increases.

However, efficiency does not arise spontaneously — it must be incentivized. **Chaabouni, Kharitonov, Dupoux, and Baroni (NeurIPS 2019)** showed that without brevity pressure, neural agents produce **anti-efficient** codes violating Zipf's Law of Abbreviation. **Rita, Chaabouni, and Dupoux (CoNLL 2020)** demonstrated that near-optimal codes emerge only when both speaker "laziness" (length penalty) and listener "impatience" (incremental guessing) are combined — a design principle directly applicable to Trasgo's codec optimization.

For compositionality, **Chaabouni, Kharitonov, Bouchacourt, Dupoux, and Baroni (ACL 2020)** found that compositionality aids **language transmission** (new agents learn compositional codes faster) but shows **no correlation with generalization**. **Galke et al. (Nature Communications 2024)** confirmed that both GPT-3.5 and humans benefit from compositional language structure, suggesting LLM codecs should leverage compositionality for learnability.

Most relevantly for LLM-to-LLM communication, **Marro et al. (arXiv 2024)** proposed the Agora protocol, identifying the "Agent Communication Trilemma" (versatility, efficiency, portability) and showing LLMs can **transition from verbose natural language to efficient standardized routines** through interaction. **Ashery, Artime, and Baronchelli (Science Advances, 2025)** demonstrated that populations of 24–200 LLM agents spontaneously develop **universally adopted social conventions** through decentralized interaction — a Lewis signaling game result at LLM scale. The original framework from **Lewis (Harvard University Press, 1969)** shows how arbitrary but efficient conventions emerge through coordination pressure, while the RSA framework of **Frank and Goodman (Science, 2012)** formalizes communication as recursive Bayesian reasoning optimizing information transfer — precisely the objective of a compressed codec.

## Conclusion: a convergent theoretical foundation

The theoretical landscape reveals seven convergent threads supporting Trasgo's core claims. In-context learning is not mere pattern matching — it implements functional gradient descent in RKHS (Cheng et al., ICML 2024), achieving near-minimax-optimal rates (Bai et al., NeurIPS 2023). Grammar induction is demonstrably within transformer capabilities (Allen-Zhu and Li, ICLR 2025), and structured grammar prompting already works in practice (Wang et al., NeurIPS 2023). The prediction-compression equivalence (Delétang et al., ICLR 2024) means that any structured representation achieving lower cross-entropy than natural language is, by formal identity, a better compressor — and LLMs' proven simplicity bias (Goldblum et al., ICML 2024) means they prefer such representations. The MDL framework provides the formal criterion: a codec is justified when L(schema) + L(data|schema) < L(natural language). Fiber bundle geometry offers the most sophisticated formalism for structured context, though its application to LLM representations remains an open contribution. And multi-agent communication theory predicts that information-pressured agents converge to exactly the kind of minimal, convention-based codes that Trasgo describes.

The two most significant theoretical gaps for the thesis to address are: (1) no existing work proves formal bounds on codec induction sample complexity — how many in-context examples suffice, and for what class of codecs; and (2) the fiber bundle formalization of transformer context representations is entirely novel territory. These gaps represent the thesis's primary theoretical contribution opportunities.
